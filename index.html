Dictionary of Lakȟóta Mythology

    • A

    • Anúŋg Ité

      Double Faced (a vicious mythical character with a different face on each side of the head). The Double Woman.

    • Aŋpétu

      Day, daytime, light, daylight.

    • Até

      Father.

    • B

    • Bdewákhaŋthuŋwaŋ

      Dwellers at the Sacred Lake (Spirit Lake). Eastern Dakota Tribe, one of four Santee-Dakota tribes. The older form is Mdewákhaŋthuŋwaŋ.

    • Č

    • Čháǧa

      Ice.

    • Čháŋ

      Tree, wodd, stick.

    • ČhaŋágnakA

      To put somone on a burial scaffold. Čhaŋágnakapi Funeral Stand, burrial scaffold. In the old days the body of a deceased person was wrapped in hides and placed on a wooden platform set on high posts.

    • Čháŋčheǧa

      Drum.

    • Čhaŋgléška

      Hoop, wheel; circle. A hoop is an important symbol in Lakota belief system, as it represents the univese as well as the unity of the Lakota nation.

    • Čhaŋkú

      Road, path, trail.

    • Čhaŋnúŋpa

      A pipe. The pipe is sacred. In all that the Native people do, they used the pipe.

    • Čhaŋté

      The heart (of men and animals). Feelings, emotions, affections, sentiments. Followed by various verbs it is used idiomatically for expressing numerous feelings and emotional states.

    • E

    • Ečíyapi

      To be called (he or she it is called).

    • Eháke

      For the last time, as the last, lastly.

    • Eháŋni

      Long ao, at some time in the past, formerly, earlier, in the old days or times.

    • Eyá

      To say something. The West Wind.

    • G

    • Gnáški

      The Demon. Son of Únk and Íya.

    • GnaškíŋyAŋ

      To be insane, crazy, frantic, wild, frenzied, go berserk, raging mad. Walker describes the evolution of the demon into the Crazy Buffalo.

    • GnáyAŋ

      To play tricks on somebody, trick sombeody, fool, deceive, cheat against somebody, break one’s own word to somebody, convince, persuade, influence.

    • Ǧ

    • Ǧáŋ

      To have untidy or unkept hair, bushy hair, uncombed, disheveled.

    • Ǧí

      To be brown.

    • Ǧú

      To be burned, scorched.

    • H

    • Haŋhépi

      Darkness, night.

    • Haŋwí

      The moon. The feminine Sun.

    • Heyókȟa

      Trickster, contrary. The other self of Wakíŋyaŋ, who under this aspect is the spirit of growth and birth, and is called by Walker “the Restorer.” Thunder dreamer, a person who dreams of thunder beings and is obliged to perform ceremonies and customs connected with the heyókȟa society.

    • Huŋká

      Relative, ancestor, a person adopted or honored in a húŋká ceremony.

    • Huŋkáke

      To tell a myth, tell a bedtime story.

    • Húŋkpapȟa

      Head of the camp circle, end of horn. One of the seven Lakota tribes.

    • Húŋku

      Mother.

    • Hú Núŋpa

      Two legs. The Bear.

    • Ȟ

    • Ȟé

      Mountain, mountain ridge.

    • Ȟtayétu

      Evening.

    • ȞugnáǧA

      To be consumed by fire, burned up, burned down.

    • Ȟ́’áŋ

      To have done it, to have committed a deed, to have taken a course of action, to have done "a thing to," to do.

    • Ȟ’aŋyÁŋ

      To be dying, be sinking away, suffering extremely, be frantic with pain, have excrutiating pain, in agony.

    • I

    • Ibóm

      The Cyclone. A name for Íya at his most violent

    • Iháŋktȟuŋwaŋ

      End detachment. Yankton Western Dakota tribe.

    • Iháŋktȟuŋwwaŋna

      Little end detachment. Yanktonai Western Dakota tribe.

    • Ikčé

      Common, natural, real. Ikčé Oyáte.

    • Iktómi

      Spider. The Trickster.

    • Íŋyaŋ

      Stone.

    • Itázipčho

      No-bow, Sans Arc. One of the seven Lakota tribes.

    • Ité

      Face.

    • Íya

      Monster.

    • Inípiti

      Sweat lodge.

    • K

    • Káŋ

      Strange, wonderful, ancient, sacred. To tell a myth.

    • Khéya

      Turtle.

    • KsápA

      Wisdom. To be wise, smart, prudent, bright. To be chaste and virtuous in woman's ways.

    • K’sápela

      Little Wisdom.

    • M

    • Makȟá

      Earth, the World. Land. Nature. Dirt, soil, dust.

    • Makȟá Akáŋ

      Earth spirit.

    • Mnikȟówožu

      Plant near water. One of seven Lakota tribes.

    • Mniwátú

      Stagnant water. Water monster. A whale.

    • N

    • Naǧí

      Spirit. The naǧí, naǧila, niyá, and sičúŋ are the four aspects of the spirit or soul. Walker calls the naǧí “the spirit that advises the conduct of each being.” Amiotte calls it the ghost or double, and according to George Swoard, “the naǧí of an animate being is its spirit and of an inanimate thing that grows from the ground is its smoke. This is the potency of anything.” It is given by Škáŋ.

    • Naǧíla

      Spirit. The particle of the energy of Táku Škáŋškáŋ which is in each being.

    • Niyá

      To breathe. “The life breath of a being” (Amiotte). “Ghost that is the breath of life and witness to the advice the spirit gives” (Walker). Given by Taté.

    • O

    • Očhéthi Šakówiŋ

      The Seven Council Fires. The confederation of Lakota and Dakota tribes.

    • Oglála

      Scatter their own. One of the seven Lakota tribes.

    • Ókaǧa

      The South Wind. Itókaǧata means south, southward, to the south, in the south.

    • O’óhenuŋpa

      Two kettles, boil twice. One of seven Lakota tribes.

    • Oyáte

      A people, nation.

    • P

    • Páhiŋ

      Porcupine.

    • Pté Oyáte

      Buffalo people.

    • S

    • Sičháŋǧu

      Burnt thighes, brulés. One of seven Lakota tribes.

    • Sičúŋ

      Spirit. “That in man which is spriti and guards him against evel spirits.” (Buechel). The power to produce offspring, health, and growth (Walker). The indiviudal energy-power peculiar to each being (Amiotte). Given by Wakíŋyaŋ/Heyókȟa.

    • Sihásapa

      Black foot, black feet. One of seven Lakota tribes.

    • Sisíthuŋwaŋ

      Slimy detachment. Sesseton tribe of Dakota, one of the four tribes of Eastern Dakotas.

    • Š

    • Škáŋ

      The active, energy. “To do, to act, to move about” (Buechel). According to Walker, Škáŋ is the shamanic term for Táku Škaŋškaŋ, and is the sky but also an omnipresent Spirit that gives life and motion to every living thing.

    • Šúŋk

      Dog.

  • T

  • Takú Škáŋškaŋ

    The Great Spirit. “That which causes everything to move” (Buechel).

  • Táŋka

    Great.

  • Tatáŋka

    Buffalo bull, bison.

  • Tatúyetopa

    The four quarters.

  • Tȟaté

    The wind.

  • Thítȟuŋwaŋ

    The plains detachment. Teton(general name for all seven Lakota tribes). Usage: not common now, the name Lakȟóta is more frequent. Transcribed as Teton in most literature. Before the migration from Minnesota in 17th century, this was probably the name of a single tribe, which divided into seven tribal groups during the migration or after crossing west of the Missouri.

  • Tokȟáhe

    The first.

  • Tokȟapa

    First-born.

  • U

  • Uŋȟčéǧi

    Prehistoric animal, monster.

  • Úŋk

    Walker says she is Passion, or Dissension.

  • Uŋktéhi

    Water monsters, the offspring of Úŋk and Uŋȟčéǧi.

  • W

  • Waȟpékhute

    Leafshooters. One of four Eastern Dakota tribes.

  • Waȟpéthuŋwaŋ

    Leaf Dwellers. One of four Easter Dakota tribes.

  • WakáŋkA

    Old or elderly person, an elder. The Old Woman.

  • Wakȟáŋ

    To be endowed with spiritual power, be sacred, holy, spiritual. To be mysterious, incomprehensible, magical. The sacred spiritual power. Sacredly, magically, spiritually, in a holy manner.

  • Wakȟáŋ Tȟáŋka

    The universal spiritual power variably translated as God, Creator, Great Spirit. The Great Mysterious, the Great Incomprehensible.

  • Wakíŋyaŋ

    Thunder. Thunder-being, thunderbird, the flying one. The dual Spirit, under this aspect he is terrifying and destructive, but his other self, Heyóka, has the beneficent side of the laughter-loving clown.

  • Wamákȟaškaŋ

    Animal.

  • WamnáyAŋ

    To make a collection of things, gather things, collect.

  • Wanáǧi

    Spirit, ghost, soul separated from the body (unknown people’s ghosts, not a particular person’s spirit, which is naǧí).

  • Wanáǧi Tȟačhaŋku

    The Milky Way. “Ghosts road”.

  • Waníyetu

    Year.

  • Waŋblí

    Eagle.

  • Wazí

    Pine tree. The Old Man of the North, the Wizard.

  • Wí

    Sun.

  • Wó’oglakapi

    A story, tale.

  • Wó’opȟe

    Order, harmony, law, regulation, rule, custom, traditional social rules. The Mediator.

  • Y

  • Yáŋpa

    The East Wind.

  • Yáta

    The North Wind.

  • Yumnímni

    To whirl, to turn something around. The little whirlwind or dust devil.

  • Z

  • Zí

    Yellow.

  • Zuzéča

    Snake.
